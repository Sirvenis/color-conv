Color Theory Basics Every Designer Should Know

Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 8 min

Color theory is the foundation of visual design. Understanding how colors work together — and against each other — helps you create designs that are not just beautiful but also functional and accessible.

The Color Wheel

The color wheel is a circular arrangement of colors based on their chromatic relationship. It starts with three primary colors (red, yellow, blue), which combine to form secondary colors (orange, green, purple), and tertiary colors from further mixing.

Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Colors

  • Primary: Red, Yellow, Blue — cannot be created by mixing other colors
  • Secondary: Orange, Green, Purple — made by mixing two primaries
  • Tertiary: Red-orange, Yellow-green, Blue-purple, etc. — mixing a primary with an adjacent secondary

Color Harmony

Color harmony refers to combinations that are aesthetically pleasing. The main schemes are:

  • Complementary: Colors opposite on the wheel (blue + orange). High contrast, high energy.
  • Analogous: Colors next to each other (blue + blue-green + green). Harmonious, serene.
  • Triadic: Three equally spaced colors (red + yellow + blue). Vibrant, balanced.
  • Split-complementary: A color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement. Less tension than complementary.
  • Monochromatic: One hue with varying saturation and lightness. Clean, cohesive.

Warm vs. Cool Colors

Colors on the red-orange side of the wheel are warm (energetic, exciting). Colors on the blue-green side are cool (calm, professional). Warm colors advance (appear closer), cool colors recede (appear farther away).

The 60-30-10 Rule

A practical guideline for color balance: use your dominant color for 60% of the design (backgrounds), a secondary color for 30% (cards, sections), and an accent color for 10% (buttons, links, highlights). This creates visual hierarchy without overwhelming the viewer.

Conclusion

Color theory isn't just academic — it's a practical toolkit for every design decision. Start with the color wheel, choose a harmony scheme, and use the 60-30-10 rule. Your designs will immediately look more polished and intentional.
