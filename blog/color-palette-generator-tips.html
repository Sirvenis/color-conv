5 Tips for Creating Beautiful Color Palettes

Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 6 min

Choosing colors is one of the most impactful — and challenging — parts of design. These five strategies will help you build palettes that look professional and serve your project's goals.

1. Start with One Hero Color

Don't try to build a full palette at once. Start with one color that captures the mood or brand identity you want. This is your hero color — it will appear in buttons, links, and key UI elements. Once you have it, build the rest of the palette around it.

Use a color converter to get your hero color in HSL format, then create variations by adjusting saturation and lightness.

2. Use the 60-30-10 Rule

This classic interior design principle works perfectly for digital design:

  • 60% dominant: Background colors, large surfaces (usually neutral)
  • 30% secondary: Cards, sections, sidebars (can be a muted version of your hero color)
  • 10% accent: Buttons, links, highlights (your hero color at full saturation)

This ratio ensures visual hierarchy without color chaos.

3. Limit Your Palette to 3-5 Colors

More colors don't mean better design. A focused palette of 3-5 colors creates cohesion. Too many colors overwhelm users and dilute your brand identity. For most projects, you need:

  • One primary/brand color
  • One or two neutrals (dark gray, light gray)
  • One accent color for CTAs
  • One semantic color (green for success, red for errors)

4. Test for Accessibility Early

A beautiful palette that fails WCAG contrast requirements is useless. Check every text-on-background combination for at least 4.5:1 contrast ratio. Also test with color blindness simulators — your blue and green might look identical to someone with deuteranopia.

5. Steal from Nature and Art

The best color combinations already exist in nature — sunsets, forests, ocean scenes. Photographs and paintings have been refined by millions of years of evolution or centuries of artistic practice. Use an eyedropper tool on a photo you love, then use a color converter to get the exact HEX values.

Common Palette Mistakes

  • Using pure black (#000000) for text — dark gray (#1a1a2e or #333) is easier on the eyes
  • Making all colors equally saturated — use muted colors for backgrounds, vivid for accents
  • Ignoring semantic colors — users expect green=good, red=bad, yellow=warning
  • Not testing on different screens — colors look different on phones vs monitors

Tools to Help

Use a color converter to work between HEX, RGB, and HSL formats. HSL is especially useful because you can keep the same hue and adjust saturation/lightness to create harmonious variations.

Conclusion

Great palettes aren't random — they're intentional. Start with one hero color, follow the 60-30-10 rule, limit your palette, test for accessibility, and draw inspiration from the world around you.
