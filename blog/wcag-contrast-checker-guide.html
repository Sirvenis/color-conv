WCAG Contrast Checker Guide: Making Your Designs Accessible

Published: April 2026 | Reading time: 7 min

About 300 million people worldwide have some form of color vision deficiency. WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) contrast requirements ensure your text is readable by everyone, regardless of visual ability.

What Is WCAG?

WCAG is a set of international standards for web accessibility published by the W3C. Contrast ratio is one of its most important requirements, measuring the difference in luminance (perceived brightness) between foreground and background colors.

Most websites should aim for AA compliance. AAA is recommended for critical content like health information or legal documents.

Common Contrast Mistakes

  • Light gray text on white backgrounds: Very common in modern minimalist design. Often fails AA.
  • Colored text on colored backgrounds: Yellow on light green, blue on purple — hard to read for many users.
  • Placeholder text: Often too light to meet standards. Ensure placeholders meet AA even though they're not final content.
  • Disabled states: Elements still need to be identifiable even when disabled.

How to Check Contrast

Use a color converter with contrast checking. Enter your foreground and background colors, and the tool calculates the exact contrast ratio and tells you whether it passes AA or AAA.

Practical Tips for Accessible Color Choices

  1. Use dark text on light backgrounds (or vice versa) — the simplest way to guarantee good contrast
  2. Don't rely on color alone to convey meaning — add icons, text labels, or patterns
  3. Test your palette early in the design process, not as an afterthought
  4. Consider users with color blindness — about 8% of men have red-green deficiency
  5. Use tools that simulate different types of color blindness

Beyond Text: UI Components

Contrast requirements also apply to UI elements — form field borders, button backgrounds, icons, and focus indicators. A button with a 2:1 contrast ratio against its background is hard to identify for users with low vision.

Color Blindness and Design

The three main types of color blindness affect how people perceive colors:

  • Protanopia: Reduced red perception
  • Deuteranopia: Reduced green perception (most common)
  • Tritanopia: Reduced blue perception (rare)

Use patterns, labels, and sufficient contrast to ensure information is accessible to all users.

Conclusion

Accessible design isn't optional — it's a legal requirement in many jurisdictions and the right thing to do. Check your contrast ratios, test for color blindness, and make your content readable for everyone.
